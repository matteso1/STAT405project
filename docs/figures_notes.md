# Figure-by-figure notes

Prep notes for talking through each plot in the presentation and
report. All figures live in `figures/`, generated by
`src/visualize.py` from `results/daily_pooled.csv`,
`results/event_study.csv`, `results/changepoints.csv`, and
`results/regression_coefs.csv`.

---

## 01_sentiment_timeline.png — "Daily VADER sentiment of Russia-Ukraine tweets (English)"

**What it shows.** X-axis is calendar date (Feb 2022 to June 2023,
476 days). Y-axis is the mean compound VADER score (-1 to +1) for
all English tweets on that day. The thin pale line is the raw
daily mean; the thick blue line is a 7-day rolling mean.
Teal dashed verticals are pro-Ukraine-narrative events, orange
dashed are pro-Russia-narrative events (from `events/events.csv`).
Red solid verticals are structural breaks found by PELT
change-point detection run on the daily mean with the L2 cost
function and penalty 10.

**Reading the plot.**

- The daily mean sits in the -0.05 to -0.15 band most of the
  time. VADER is scoring the average tweet as mildly negative.
  That fits: the tweets are about an ongoing war. The zero line
  is mostly above the series.
- Two clear negative excursions: (a) a dip in early April 2022
  (around the Bucha revelation, when news of civilian killings
  broke as Russia withdrew from around Kyiv); (b) the deepest
  excursion of the whole series is in April-May 2023, which is
  the Bakhmut period (the city fell / Wagner claimed full
  capture on 2023-05-20).
- Some small positive spikes sit near Zelensky's December 2022
  address to the U.S. Congress and around Ukrainian counter-
  offensive news in autumn 2022. These are days when "support",
  "thank", "liberat" etc. outnumber the usual war vocabulary.
- The red change-point lines are dense. PELT fires on anything
  that looks like a level shift in the daily mean; most of these
  breaks do *not* line up with dashed event lines. This is the
  setup for the takeaway in the report: PELT and our event list
  are mostly orthogonal.

**What to say out loud (presentation).**

> "This is every day of the war on English Twitter. The blue line
> is the 7-day rolling VADER sentiment. It sits around minus 0.1
> most of the time. Two dips stand out — Bucha in April 2022 and
> the Bakhmut period in spring 2023. The red lines are structural
> breaks detected independently by PELT; most of them don't line
> up with our labeled events. That's one of the main tensions in
> the results: the algorithm is finding structure we didn't label."

## 02_event_study.png — "Change in daily mean sentiment: 7 days after vs. 7 days before event"

**What it shows.** Horizontal bar chart, one bar per event. Bar
length is `mean_post - mean_pre`, where `mean_post` is the
average daily mean compound in the 7 days after the event and
`mean_pre` is the same for the 7 days before (excluding the
event day itself). Bar color encodes narrative valence
(pro-UA = teal, pro-RU = orange, neutral = grey). Events with a
Welch-*t* whose |*t*| > 2 have a label like `t=2.7` next to the
bar. Only 16 of the 21 events are plotted — five events
(Wagner mutiny through Avdiivka falls) fall past the end of our
data window and don't have a full 7-day post-window.

**Reading the plot.**

- **Three events clear |*t*| > 2.**
    - *Kharkiv counter-offensive* (Sep 2022): +0.04, *t* = 2.7.
      Sentiment went more positive after Ukraine's rapid
      advances. That's the expected sign for a pro-UA narrative
      event — "liberated", "victory" style language.
    - *Annexation declared* (Sep 2022): +0.02, *t* = 2.4. More
      positive after, which is the *opposite* of what you might
      naively expect for a pro-RU narrative event. VADER is
      probably picking up condemnation-with-positive-framing
      ("stand with Ukraine", "support") more than the RU
      narrative itself.
    - *Bucha revealed* (Apr 2022): -0.08, *t* = -2.2. Sentiment
      dropped after the news broke. Expected sign: the vocabulary
      shifts toward "killed", "mass", "atrocity".
- **Everything else is inside the |*t*| < 2 band.** The Bakhmut
  claim bar is positive, which looks weird given how deep the
  Bakhmut-period trough is in figure 1 — but the event-study
  window is 7+7 days centered on 2023-05-20, and the trough
  sits mostly *before* the claim. So the "post minus pre"
  delta doesn't capture the whole dip.
- **Why events cluster near zero.** A 7-day window averages over
  ~7 independent daily means; VADER daily means have SD of
  roughly 0.04-0.06 on top of a mean near -0.1. So the minimum
  detectable shift with |*t*| > 2 is roughly 0.04-0.06. Most
  events just don't move the English-Twitter daily mean that
  much.

**What to say out loud.**

> "This is an event-study view. Each bar is post-minus-pre, with
> a 7-day window. Three events clear *t* equals two: Kharkiv
> counter-offensive goes positive, annexation goes positive,
> Bucha goes negative. That's three out of sixteen — not a lot,
> and it tells us English Twitter as a whole doesn't shift a
> huge amount around most single events, at least not on a
> one-week timescale."

## 03_volume_vs_sentiment.png — "Tweet volume and sentiment, English"

**What it shows.** Two stacked panels sharing an x-axis (date).

- Top: tweets per day on a log scale. Volume starts around
  3×10⁵/day in late Feb 2022 right after the invasion, falls
  gradually through summer 2022, drops a level in August 2022,
  rises briefly around Feb 2023 (one-year anniversary + Zelensky
  activity), and drops sharply in late May 2023.
- Bottom: daily mean compound score (linear scale, ~-0.3 to
  +0.2).

**Reading the plot.**

- The volume drops aren't our model's story — they're the
  dataset's. The Kaggle dump was scraped on particular days and
  the collection mechanism changed (Twitter API pricing shifted
  during the period). That's a collection artifact, not a
  statement about public interest.
- Because daily means are computed over smaller samples in low-
  volume periods (especially late May 2023), their variance goes
  up. The deepest-looking dips in the bottom panel are on days
  where `n_tweets` is two orders of magnitude below peak — so
  they're noisier. This is exactly the `log(n_tweets)` control
  in the regression: it soaks up the volume effect rather than
  letting it masquerade as sentiment.
- Sentiment in the bottom panel doesn't track volume in any
  obvious way. High-volume early days are only mildly negative;
  very low-volume late-period days are more negative but also
  more variable.

**What to say out loud (if you have time for it).**

> "This panel is a sanity check. Top is tweet volume on log
> scale; bottom is daily mean sentiment. The volume dropoffs are
> the dataset's collection artifacts, not public-interest decay.
> The regression controls for log-volume so we don't mistake
> small-sample noise for a real sentiment shift."

## 04_language_heatmap.png — "Weekly mean sentiment by language (top 10 by volume)"

**What it shows.** Heatmap. Rows are the 10 languages with the
highest total tweet count (`en, und, de, fr, it, es, uk, ru, tr,
ja`). Columns are weeks (from Feb 2022 to June 2023). Color is
weekly mean compound score, diverging red (positive) to blue
(negative), centered at zero. `und` is the `und` language code
for "undetermined" (Twitter couldn't detect the language).

**Reading the plot (carefully — this is a gotcha).**

- **The biggest story is that German looks extremely negative**
  (the `de` row is deep blue across the whole period). That is
  *not* because German-speaking Twitter is uniquely sad about
  the war — it's because **VADER is trained on English**.
  German words are unfamiliar tokens to VADER, which then picks
  up on whatever English words leak through (swear words in
  reposted English content, for example) and over-weights them.
  Same mechanism explains why Italian and Japanese can look
  slightly *positive*: the VADER English vocabulary fails to
  fire at all on most tokens, so the score defaults toward
  neutral and any positive-word accidents dominate.
- We show this plot to document the limitation, not to make
  claims about non-English sentiment. The report explicitly
  says VADER is English-only and that the multilingual pool is
  a sensitivity check, not a primary result.

**What to say out loud (if asked).**

> "Language heatmap. I want to be upfront: the strong colors
> here are mostly VADER failing on non-English tokens, not
> actual differences in how each language-community feels about
> the war. We kept the plot in to make the limitation concrete,
> but all the statistical results in the paper are English-only."

## 05_reach_weighted.png — "Does who's shouting change the story? (7-day rolling)"

**What it shows.** Two overlaid 7-day rolling lines on the same
date axis.

- Blue solid: tweet-weighted daily mean (every tweet counts
  once — same as figure 1).
- Orange dashed: follower-weighted daily mean. Each tweet is
  weighted by the tweeter's follower count, so a post from an
  account with 1M followers counts 1000× more than one with
  1000 followers. Computed from `sum_followers_weighted_compound
  / sum_followers`.

**Reading the plot.**

- The two lines **track each other in sign** almost everywhere.
  When the blue line dips, the orange dashed line dips too.
  When the blue rises (e.g. Zelensky at Congress), the orange
  tends to rise too.
- The follower-weighted line is **noisier** and sometimes dips
  deeper — it hits roughly -0.2 more often than the blue line
  does, and has bigger swings in low-volume periods where a
  few big accounts can dominate. But it doesn't systematically
  live above or below the blue line.
- **Interpretation.** High-follower accounts aren't collectively
  saying something different from the median tweet. They're
  saying the same thing, louder. For our research question —
  "does sentiment shift around events?" — the answer doesn't
  change if you weight by reach. If we'd seen the follower-
  weighted line track above zero while the tweet-weighted line
  tracked below, that would mean influencers were systematically
  more positive than the crowd. We don't see that.

**What to say out loud.**

> "Last plot is a robustness check. Blue is the simple tweet
> average. Orange dashed weights each tweet by the author's
> follower count, so big accounts count more. The two lines
> track in sign everywhere. Amplifier effects shift the amplitude
> but not the direction. So the sentiment story doesn't change
> if we privilege high-reach accounts."

## Cross-figure links for the talk

- Figure 1 shows the deepest dip is Bakhmut-period (spring 2023),
  but figure 2's event-study bar for Bakhmut claim is small and
  positive. That's because the event-study window (7+7 days
  around 2023-05-20) misses most of the trough, which sat
  mostly *before* the claim date.
- Figure 1 has ~21 red change-points; figure 2 identifies only
  3 events with |*t*| > 2. The regression's 4 significant
  coefficients (Kherson liberated, Kakhovka dam, anniversary,
  Zelensky) are a slightly different set because the regression
  uses a full-series model instead of a 14-day window. Don't get
  tripped up — the three analyses are answering slightly
  different questions.
- Figure 5 (reach-weighting) is the answer to an obvious
  audience question: "what about bots / influencers?" The plot
  shows reach-weighting doesn't change the qualitative story,
  which is why the report's limitation mentions bots only as
  future work, not as a threat to the current findings.
